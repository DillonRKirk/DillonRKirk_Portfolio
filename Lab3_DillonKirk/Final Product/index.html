Homicide Rates as Huītzilōpōchtli

Dillon Kirk

Mexico is a developing nation-state best known for its rich cultural history, delicious cuisine, and exquisite vacation destinations. However, Mexico has been plagued by exploding homicide rates since President Felipe Calderón declared war on narcoterrorism in 2006. While global averages of homicides per 100,000 residents have averaged between 5.4 and 7.0 from 2000 to 2020 (World Bank, see left), several of Mexico’s cities have seen greater than 100 homicides per 100,000 residents during the same time period. While one can argue that Mexico’s exploding homicide rates can be attributed to its war on narcoterrorism, one can also argue that certain cultural factors facilitate its rise.

Compared to its peers, Mexican culture has a unique historical relationship with the concept of death and sacrifice. The Aztec Empire’s embrace of human sacrifice is perhaps highlighted most notably by Huītzilōpōchtli, the Aztec war deity of sacrifice. Ceremonial human sacrifices to Huītzilōpōchtli were common occurrences in Aztec society, and Huītzilōpōchtli was featured in nearly every Aztec pantheon (Meehan 2022). More on Huītzilōpōchtli can be found here. Modern Mexicans still embrace the indigenous holiday of Day of the Dead, in which participants place offerings atop alters for spirits visiting our world (Ward 2022). A rise in neopaganism has accompanied Mexican narcoterrorism, perhaps best highlighted by the rise of La Santa Muerte (Holy Death, see left). According to some estimates, La Santa Muerte has courted approximately 12 million followers (Tucker 2017), some of which conduct offerings of animal and human sacrifice (Bunker 2013).

Because of Mexico’s unique historical relationship with the concept of death and sacrifice, I chose to represent Mexico’s intentional homicide rates in a Huītzilōpōchtli theme.

Water is represented as a turquoise color with RGB of (39, 135, 137).

Land is represented in a green color with RGB of (44, 76, 59).

Hillshade is represented as a brown color with RGB of (139, 69, 19).

Road networks are represented as an orange color with RGB of (255, 129, 0).

Administrative boundaries are represented with a solid white line, regional boundaries are represented with dashed white lines, and disputed boundaries are represented with dashed yellow lines (RGB of 255, 201, 33).

Lastly, fonts are written in the Nosifer style, which is a blood-dripping style, in either white for natural point labels or blood red for place labels (RGB of 227, 26, 28).
